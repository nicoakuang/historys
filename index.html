Remembering the Generalissimo:
The Legacy of Chiang Kai-shek

Memorial Day and the Qīngmíng Tradition

Every April 5th, the Republic of China (Taiwan) not only observes National Tomb-Sweeping Day (民族掃墓節 / Mínzú Sǎomùjié), commonly known as the Qingming Festival, but also commemorates the Memorial Day for the Late President Chiang Kai-shek (先總統蔣公逝世紀念日 / Xiān Zǒngtǒng Jiǎnggōng Shìshì Jìniànrì). This date marks the passing of President Chiang Kai-shek on April 5, 1975 (the 64th year of the Republic of China). This national commemoration is a tribute to his invaluable contributions and sacrifices for the nation and the Chinese people.

This article will delve into the history, contributions, and enduring legacy of Chiang Kai-shek, one of the most influential figures in modern Chinese history, whose role is inextricably linked to the establishment and continuation of the Republic of China.

Historical Background: China in the Early 20th Century

The early 20th century in China was marked by extreme political turmoil. The collapsing Qing Dynasty, which fell in 1912, was replaced by a nascent Republic of China, yet its central authority remained weak. Warlords (軍閥 / jūnfá) controlled various regions, while foreign powers continued to exert their influence through concessions and unequal treaties. Amidst this chaos, the revolutionary and nationalist ideals championed by Dr. Sun Yat-sen and the Kuomintang (中國國民黨 / Zhōngguó Guómíndǎng), or Chinese Nationalist Party, offered a glimmer of hope for national unity and independence.

It was in this context that Chiang Kai-shek emerged as a military and political leader who would inherit the torch of struggle from Sun Yat-sen, confronting internal challenges from political factions and the nascent communist movement, as well as external threats from an increasingly aggressive Imperial Japan.

Profile of a Key Figure: Generalissimo Chiang Kai-shek (蔣中正 / Jiǎng Zhōngzhèng)

Born in 1887 in Zhejiang Province, Chiang Kai-shek was a pivotal military and political leader who became the successor to Dr. Sun Yat-sen. He led the Kuomintang (Chinese Nationalist Party), unified China through the Northern Expedition, and guided the nation through the Second Sino-Japanese War. Following his defeat in the Chinese Civil War in 1949, he relocated the Republic of China government to Taiwan, where he continued to serve as President until his death. His role in modern Chinese history is both crucial and complex.

Chiang Kai-shek's Major Contributions to the Nation and People

1. Unification of China

Although the Republic of China was established on January 1, 1912, much of the northern region remained under warlord control, far removed from the principles of the Three Principles of the People (三民主義 / Sānmín Zhǔyì: Nationalism, Democracy, and People's Livelihood) advocated by Dr. Sun Yat-sen. Sun founded the Whampoa Military Academy (黃埔軍校 / Huángpǔ Jūnxiào) in 1924, appointing Chiang Kai-shek as its commandant. Following Sun's passing on March 12, 1925, Chiang assumed leadership. On July 9, 1926, Chiang was inaugurated as the Commander-in-Chief of the National Revolutionary Army, leading the Northern Expedition (北伐 / Běifá) from Guangzhou. By the end of 1928, the Northern Expedition succeeded, nominally unifying China under the Republic of China government, with Nanjing (南京 / Nánjīng) as its capital.

2. Construction of China: The "Nanjing Decade"

Following unification, China under Chiang's Nationalist government faced immense challenges from Japanese invasion and internal struggles with the Chinese Communist Party (CCP). Japan, long coveting Chinese territory, launched a series of military aggressions, ranging from the occupation of Qingdao (1914) and the Jinan Incident (1928) to the occupation of Manchuria via the September 18th Incident (九一八事變 / Jiǔyībā Shìbiàn) in 1931, and the establishment of the puppet state Manchukuo (偽滿州國 / Wěimǎnzhōuguó) in 1932. Concurrently, the Soviet Union intervened, supporting the CCP. Responding to this dual threat, Chiang's government adopted the policy of "Stabilize the Interior, Resist Foreign Aggression" (安內攘外 / Ānnèi Rǎngwài), meaning to stabilize the internal situation while preparing to confront Japanese aggression.

The period from the completion of the Northern Expedition in 1928 to the outbreak of the Second Sino-Japanese War on July 7, 1937 (the Marco Polo Bridge Incident / 盧溝橋事變 / Lúgōuqíáo Shìbiàn) is known as the "Nanjing Decade" (南京十年 / Nánjīng Shínián) or the "Golden Decade." During this time, the Nationalist government strived to build a modern China. They implemented the Five-Power Constitution (五權憲法 / Wǔquán Xiànfǎ) system, developed local autonomy, strengthened the army, established air and naval forces, advanced military industries, stabilized finance, developed education, revised laws, promoted industry, agriculture, and trade, built transportation infrastructure, conducted land reform, protected labor rights, improved healthcare, and developed private media. Historians argue that had it not been for the Japanese invasion, China would have become a prosperous, strong, free, and democratic nation long before 1949.

3. Leading the War of Resistance Against Japan

Chiang Kai-shek led the entire military and populace of China in a heroic eight-year struggle against Japanese aggression. This was a victory unprecedented in thousands of years of Chinese history—unlike previous dynasties that had succumbed to invaders. Despite numerous voices of pessimism and surrender, Chiang, through his personal integrity and charisma, successfully rallied the entire nation to unite against the aggression. His leadership during this War of Resistance (抗戰 / Kàngzhàn) is considered monumental for the survival of the Chinese nation.

4. Abolition of Unequal Treaties

In his last will and testament, Dr. Sun Yat-sen called for "a national assembly to be convened and unequal treaties to be abolished as soon as possible." At that time, abolishing these treaties was an incredibly daunting task given China's still-weakened state. However, under the leadership of Chiang Kai-shek, China persevered in the war. When World War II erupted and Japan joined the Axis Powers in 1940, some European powers shifted from adversaries to allies. After Japan attacked Pearl Harbor and the United States declared war on Japan, China became one of the major Allied powers and was recognized as one of the "Big Four" world powers.

On January 11, 1943, the United States and Great Britain respectively signed treaties with China to abolish extraterritorial rights in China and resolve related issues. Belgium, Norway, Canada, Sweden, the Netherlands, France, Switzerland, Denmark, and Portugal subsequently followed suit, relinquishing their unequal treaties. Dr. Sun Yat-sen's dying wish was finally realized, and the unequal treaties from the late Qing Dynasty were abolished. However, the unequal treaties with Russia remain unresolved to this day, given Soviet support for the CCP which eventually overthrew the Republic of China on the mainland.

5. Building and Defending Taiwan

After the defeat in the Chinese Civil War in 1949, Chiang Kai-shek led approximately one million freedom-loving civilians and military personnel to retreat to Taiwan. Together with millions of Taiwanese, they built the island's military, economic, and social foundations. The Republic of China government in Taiwan adhered to the Three Principles of the People and the Five-Power Constitution, establishing Taiwan as a base for national recovery and defending "Free China." By the 1990s, the Republic of China's economy in Taiwan was widely recognized as one of the "Four Asian Tigers," and to this day, Taiwan remains the sole free territory under the Republic of China government.

6. Reviving Chinese Culture

After seizing power in 1949, the CCP incessantly launched political campaigns. During the three decades on the mainland, an estimated 70 to 100 million people died due to executions or man-made disasters, three times the number of military and civilian casualties during the Sino-Japanese War. In May 1966, the CCP launched the ten-year-long "Cultural Revolution" (文化大革命 / Wénhuà Dàgémìng), aiming to eradicate traditional Chinese ethics and culture.

In response, in November 1966, fifteen hundred political and cultural figures in the Republic of China initiated the Chinese Cultural Renaissance Movement (中華文化復興運動 / Zhōnghuá Wénhuá Fùxīng Yùndòng). A committee was formed the following year, with Chiang Kai-shek serving as its president. In a time of existential crisis for Chinese culture, Chiang, from the small island of Taiwan, successfully preserved and revived China's rich cultural heritage. His contribution to the preservation of Chinese culture is considered immortal for the Chinese nation.

Modern Commemoration: "Generalissimo, We Miss You!"

To honor President Chiang's extraordinary contributions, various commemorative events are held. For instance, on April 5, 2019, a large commemorative conference titled "Generalissimo, We Miss You!" (蔣公，我們懷念您！/ Jiǎnggōng, Wǒmen Huáiniàn Nín!) was held at the National Dr. Sun Yat-sen Memorial Hall. The event included introductions of overseas Chinese returning from five continents, speeches by distinguished guests, a conference declaration, academic discussions on Chiang's life and deeds, and performances by choirs, dance troupes, and honor guards. Thousands attended, including hundreds of patriotic overseas Chinese, making it the largest commemoration of Chiang Kai-shek in decades.

This memorial day is not only a time to remember the passing of a leader but also to reflect on the complex and profound legacy he left behind—from unifying a divided nation, building a modern state, leading a pivotal war, to preserving and revitalizing an endangered culture. Chiang Kai-shek remains an inseparable figure in the narrative of the modern Republic of China.

Key Terms & Glossary

Chiang Kai-shek (蔣中正 / Jiǎng Zhōngzhèng)

►

A prominent Chinese Nationalist political and military leader who served as the leader of the Republic of China between 1928 and 1975. He was instrumental in unifying China after the warlord era and leading the country during the Second Sino-Japanese War.

Kuomintang (中國國民黨 / Zhōngguó Guómíndǎng)

►

Also known as the Chinese Nationalist Party, it was founded by Sun Yat-sen and later led by Chiang Kai-shek. It played a pivotal role in the establishment of the Republic of China and was the ruling party of Taiwan for much of the 20th century.

Three Principles of the People (三民主義 / Sānmín Zhǔyì)

►

The political philosophy developed by Sun Yat-sen to make China a free, prosperous, and powerful nation. The principles are Nationalism (民族 / Mínzú), Democracy (民權 / Mínquán), and the People's Livelihood (民生 / Mínshēng).

Northern Expedition (北伐 / Běifá)

►

A military campaign launched by the Kuomintang from 1926 to 1928, led by Chiang Kai-shek, which aimed to unify China under its own control by defeating warlords and establishing the Nationalist Government in Nanjing.
